Chapter 10: The Return of the Red Moon

Seven years later.

The world had moved on.
Devgarh was no longer whispered about in newspapers or rumors.
Arjun? Kabir? No records. No photos. No police files. Just forgotten shadows of a story no one remembered living.

 But that night — the red moon returned.
Hung again over the town like a curse breaking free.

At 11:27 PM, a video went viral. Grainy footage of a hidden chamber beneath the old campus. Bloodstains. Symbols. Files marked with names that weren’t supposed to exist.

People gasped. Authorities denied. But the damage was done. For the first time, the world started asking questions.

And in a high-security psychiatric unit outside Delhi... Ishaan Kapoor watched the red moon rise from behind shatterproof glass.

He didn’t scream. He didn’t cry.
He just whispered: “They remember now.”

That night, under the cover of chaos, fire alarms, and forgotten keys — Ishaan escaped.

He ran. Limping, broken, hallucinating. But guided by purpose.

The address had come from someone anonymous. A file slid under his door days ago. No explanation. Just a photo. A building. A name. Professor S.K. Rana — retired. Living quietly under government protection.

Ishaan took the night train to Devgarh one last time.

The house was old, forgotten — like everything else in this cursed town.

The gate creaked. The windows were dark. But the door... was open.

Ishaan stepped inside. His heart thundered. On the wall: photos of students. Marked with red ink. Scribbled names: “Subject A”, “Catalyst”, “Erased”.

On the table: A mug of tea, still warm.

He followed the corridor. Down into a study lined with steel cabinets and sealed folders.

And then — silence. Not absence of sound — but the kind of silence that waits.

Ishaan turned. And there he was.

The man was old. Frail. But his eyes… Cold. Calculating. Unmoved.

“You made it,” the man said calmly. “You were always meant to.”

Ishaan raised the knife. His hands trembling. “You erased them. You turned me into a ghost. Why!?”

The man didn’t flinch. “Because you were never supposed to remember.”

“What do you mean?” “You’re not the victim. You’re the anomaly.”

Ishaan blinked. “What…?”

“We created the Finder to trace narrative threats. You weren’t supposed to *feel*. Just observe. Record. Process.”
“But you began to love. You interfered. You broke your own rules.”

Ishaan’s mouth went dry. “I was real.” “You were useful.” “They were my friends.” “They were subjects.”

Ishaan dropped the knife. His body shook. Rage, grief, disbelief crashing together.

But when he looked up... The man was gone.
Only a red smear of blood on the chair. And a wall covered in writing:

“Truth is a story too.”
“Erase the writer, and the ending resets.”

Ishaan fell to his knees.
Alone. Again.

The red moon glowed through the window. The sirens were coming.
He had escaped the prison — only to enter another.

In the end, he wasn’t the hero. Not the survivor. Not even the finder.

Just the last one left who remembered a story no one was meant to tell.
